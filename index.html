More Than a Performance: The Importance of Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl for Latino Communities

How Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl performance spoke to love, identity and the immigrant communities living under the shadow of ICE.

By: Kirsty Farrands
Format: News feature / blog
Focus: Latino identity, visibility and immigration

Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl halftime performance was more than a pop culture moment. It became a symbol of Latin identity on a global stage, while many of the people most represented by that visibility continue to live with fear shaped by immigration enforcement and public scrutiny.

In the months leading up to the performance, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) had expanded deportation operations across the country. Arrests and raids increased, often affecting individuals with no criminal record, and reigniting fear across Latino communities. Families faced separation, and many began altering their daily routines, avoiding public spaces, work, or school in response. This made Bad Bunny's performance all the more meaningful and necessary for those Latino communities.

The Latin-inspired, Spanish-speaking performance became the 4th most viewed Super Bowl performance in history with 128.2 million live viewers in the US, while being the most watched global Super Bowl performance of all time at 4 billion views.

The 14-minute performance included a variety of Bad Bunny's most famous songs, with a vibrant, Puerto Rican-inspired set, ranging from sugar cane fields to Puerto Rican neighbourhoods in New York. The spectacle included cameos from artists such as Lady Gaga, as well as Puerto Rican singer Ricky Martin.

Symbolic Moments Celebrating Latino Immigrants

This visual highlights the layered cultural symbols used throughout the halftime show, from Puerto Rican references to neighbourhood life and immigrant identity.

Puerto Rican Culture

The performance was rich with nods to Puerto Rico. The set, designed to resemble sugar cane fields, symbolised the historic sugar and fruit farms of the island and the legacy of agricultural workers. Across the stage were smaller details, taco and coco frío stands, nail salons, scenes of boxing, and people playing dominoes, all rooted in Puerto Rican and wider Latino culture.

These elements reflect everyday cultural practices that Latino immigrants have carried with them to the United States. Over time, these traditions have become embedded within American culture itself, much like the growing presence of the Spanish language.

Puerto Rican New York

The performance also highlighted how Latino culture has travelled with immigrant communities, particularly through Puerto Rican culture in New York. Bad Bunny introduces the colourful neighbourhoods of the city through his song “NUEVAYoL” (Puerto Rican slang for New York).

New York is one of the most multicultural cities in the United States, with immigrants making up 37.9% of the current population, reflecting its long history as a centre of migration and cultural exchange.

The set included colourful storefronts such as bodegas and featured a cameo from Tonita, a well-known Puerto Rican figure in New York and owner of a historic social club. Bad Bunny referenced her in “NUEVAYoL” and has supported her venue, highlighting the importance of preserving community spaces within immigrant neighbourhoods.

Younger Benito

In the performance, a couple and their younger son are watching Bad Bunny win his Grammy, in a makeshift living room. Bad Bunny then hands his real life Grammy to the young boy, who represents younger Benito (Bad Bunny) and the younger generations.

It was speculated online that younger Benito was Liam Ramos, a 5-year-old boy who was detained by ICE, but was in fact just the actor Lincon Fox Ramadan. The stark contrast between younger Benito and Liam Ramos arguably illustrates the dream vs the reality for Latino communities in the US, where ICE has no limits on who they detain if they belong to a Latino immigrant family.

The “younger Benito” moment shifts the performance from spectacle to reflection, linking aspiration, childhood and the lived reality of immigrant families.

“Together We Are America”

After taking us through the fields of Puerto Rico, to the streets of NYC, Bad Bunny ends the performance celebrating 'The Americas'. With the flags of all the countries of North, Central and South America, he sings "God Bless America", followed by naming all the countries from the Americas, then showing an American football to the camera which says "God Bless America".

This sequence emphasised that “America” is not limited to the United States alone, but represents a collection of nations connected through migration and shared histories. The message of unity continued as the camera focused on a stadium billboard reading, “The only thing more powerful than hate is love,” echoing a statement the artist made in a recent Grammys speech, where he spoke out against immigration enforcement policies.

This section reframes “America” as a shared identity across the hemisphere rather than a single national label.

Use of Spanish

Bad Bunny chose to sing primarily in Spanish, with the only English-speaking moments being Lady Gaga's cameo and when he says “God Bless America” at the end. The Super Bowl has previously featured performers who included Spanish, such as Shakira, but not throughout an entire performance. Despite Bad Bunny regularly performing in Spanish, this marked a defining moment within the current political climate.

Spanish is becoming increasingly common in the United States, with 62% of non-English speakers in the US speak Spanish . This choice to centre Spanish on such a global stage is particularly significant.

The Reality for the Latino Community

Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl performance didn’t directly mention ICE or deportation. Still, it reflected those realities in a more symbolic, cultural way that was closely tied to what Latino communities were experiencing at the time.

The reality for immigrant families in the USA has become a terrifying and fearful one with the increased presence of ICE raids across the country. A sharp rise in ICE arrests has occurred since Trump's second term in 2024, with the Latino immigrant community being a specific target.

Over half of Latinos (52%) are fearful of the threat of deportation, according to PEW research, while 62% of these immigrants are now worried they or someone they know will be deported from the US.

The Fear Facing Latino Communities

The threat of deportation for Latino communities in the USA has become so prominent that Bad Bunny refused to tour in the USA with fear that fans attending would be targeted by ICE. This is a result of his large Spanish-speaking fanbase and popularity, particularly among Latinos.

In an interview with i-D Magazine, Benito explained, "ICE could be outside [my concert]. And it’s something that we were talking about and very concerned about." The artist has chosen to tour elsewhere across the globe, including many Latin Countries as well as European and Asian locations, at bay from ICE raids.

The tour map shows how Bad Bunny’s global routing reflects both cultural reach and the political anxieties surrounding Latino audiences in the US.

Ultimately, these symbolic moments demonstrate that the performance was never just about music, but about meaning. By centring Puerto Rican culture, Spanish language, and the shared identity of the Americas, Bad Bunny transformed one of the world’s biggest stages into a platform for visibility and belonging.

In a time of heightened political tension around immigration and identity, the performance resonated as a statement of unity and representation, showing that Latino culture is not peripheral, but central to the story of the United States itself.
